about this project



Through the act of creating a bibliography for a current internship, I found that a bibliography is more than an end-of-paper requirement for academic and professional writing. It is more than an afterthought, more than an add-on. A bibliography can be a thing in and of itself, a place to join all relevant sources on a particular topic. And when the topic at hand is the unfolding dialog surrounding a current event, the bibliography can simultaneously serve as a timeline.

When the babe.net article broke, first came an Atlantic article in response, then a Washington Post article, then a New York Times article, and another Atlantic article, and so on and so on. The articles referred to one another from the very beginning, making it necessary to backtrack a couple of articles to understand who was criticising or lauding which article. Both the content of and the dialog between the articles intrigued me, and I wanted to get a bird's eye view of this public conversation, so I created an unofficial bibliography, posted it to facebook, and solicited additions from others. The original public facebook post and subsequent discussion can be found here. I culled a large portion of the items posted on that discussion to create the bibliography page of this site, which is a work in progress, and subject to change.

In addition to explaining the use of the word “microhistory” as a classification of this mini project, the related page of this site presents two diagramming techniques that sparked my interest in thinking about the organization of claims, arguments, and opinions. As Pratt LIS student, I am often thinking about the organization of data and information, the first two levels of the traditional DKIW pyramid of Data, Inforamtion, Knowledge, and Wisdom. But what about the organization of arguments? Or opinions? Can we model a relational network of sources and each of their claims on a given topic? Could we use this process, or something similar, in modelling our own opinions on a topic by showing how we converge or diverge with others’ opinions? With these examples in mind, I am interested in how components of these diagramming techniques could be applied to the comprehension of arguments from multiple sources on a given event - like the ones presented in this site’s bibliography - and also how the components could be used in creating a diagram of one’s own argument, or a diagram of the entire landscape of the arguments of a public dialog.
